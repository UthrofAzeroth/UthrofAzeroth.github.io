About this book

Nina was supposed to send a message, nothing more. A word. A sign that the night had ended normally.

When no message comes, Mara tells herself not to overreact. Nina is an adult. Sisters fight. Phones die. People stay out. By morning the silence has taken on weight, and the details begin to resist explanation. Nina’s keys are still in her flat. Her charger is still plugged in. On the doormat lies a cream card bearing only a time, an address, and a chilling instruction: Please arrive alone.

What begins as an anxious search for a missing sister leads Mara into a world of private dinner houses, curated guest lists, and wealthy strangers who speak in the smooth language of choice, reinvention, and freedom. They insist Nina was not trapped. They insist she was changing. They insist Mara’s fear is merely the old family story refusing to let go.

The deeper Mara goes, the clearer it becomes that this is a place where harm is hidden behind hospitality, coercion is dressed as care, and truth can be rewritten before it ever reaches daylight. To get Nina back, Mara must step inside the structure that took hold of her: the house, the invitations, the rules, and the people who believe they own the meaning of what happens behind closed doors.
